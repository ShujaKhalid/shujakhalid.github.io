About

I am a PhD candidate at the University of Toronto, supervised by Prof. Frank Rudzicz, working at the intersection of machine learning and computer vision. My research centers on visual scene understanding, representation learning, and explainable AI (XAI), with a particular interest in applications to biomedical imaging.

More broadly, I enjoy building systems that connect deep learning research to practical, real-world problems — from autonomous-driving perception to cloud-scale applications and sports analytics. Below is a selection of projects spanning these areas.

Projects

Behaviour Cloning for Autonomous Driving

Deep Learning · Keras · CNNs

An end-to-end convolutional network that learns to steer a car in simulation from human driving data. Highlights how data augmentation and careful collection of recovery/turn data are critical to robust autonomous control.

Code Demo

Advanced Lane Detection

Computer Vision · OpenCV

A robust pipeline that identifies the drivable region around a vehicle by extracting maximal information from each frame to build a binary map for thresholding — substantially more resilient than a basic lane finder, and extended from images to video.

Code Demo

Traffic Sign Classification

Deep Learning · TensorFlow · LeNet

A LeNet-style CNN that classifies German traffic signs with ~95% test accuracy, rising to ~99% with data augmentation (flips, rotations) used to balance a non-uniform dataset.

Lane Detection Algorithm

Computer Vision · OpenCV

A first lane-finding pipeline combining thresholding techniques with OpenCV to detect lanes in images and video — an exploration of how environmental conditions and region-of-interest choices affect detection robustness.

Player Availability Rating (PAR)

A tool for quantifying NHL skater performance for general managers, addressing gaps in how player availability and physical attributes are reflected in conventional statistics. Built with web-scraped data and tuned regression models.
